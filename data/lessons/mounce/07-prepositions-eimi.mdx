## How Greek Prepositions Work

Greek prepositions are small words that show the **relationship** between a noun and the rest of the sentence — direction, location, source, accompaniment, etc. What makes Greek prepositions distinctive is that they **govern a specific case**, and the **meaning can shift depending on which case follows**.

For example, διά (*through, because of*):
- διά + **genitive** → *through* (means): "through faith"
- διά + **accusative** → *because of* (cause): "because of sin"

The same preposition, two different cases, two different meanings. You must learn both the preposition and which case(s) it takes.

> **ἐν** is always followed by the **dative**. It never takes another case. Engrave this on your memory.

## εἰμί — The Verb "To Be"

**εἰμί** (*I am*) is the most irregular verb in Greek, just as "to be" is in English. It appears 2,462 times in the NT. Learn these forms now; you will see them constantly.

### Present Indicative of εἰμί

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| 1st | εἰμί (*I am*) | ἐσμέν (*we are*) |
| 2nd | εἶ (*you are*) | ἐστέ (*you are*) |
| 3rd | ἐστίν (*he/she/it is*) | εἰσίν (*they are*) |

> Most forms of εἰμί are **enclitic** — they lean on the preceding word for their accent. εἰμί (1st sg.) is NOT enclitic.

## The Predicate Nominative

When εἰμί connects a subject to a description, **both** the subject and the complement are in the **nominative** case. This is the **predicate nominative**.

- ὁ λόγος ἀλήθεια **ἐστίν** — "The word **is** truth."
- Σὺ εἶ ὁ Χριστός — "You **are** the Christ."

The article typically identifies **which nominative is the subject**. When one nominative has the article and the other does not, the one with the article is usually the subject.
