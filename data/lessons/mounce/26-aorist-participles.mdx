## The Aorist Adverbial Participle

The **aorist adverbial participle** expresses an action that happened **before the main verb** (antecedent time). It answers the question: "What did the subject do *before* the main action?"

This is the most common participial construction in narrative Greek.

## Forming the Aorist Active Participle

The aorist active participle uses the **first aorist stem** (with augment removed!) + the suffix **-σας, -σασα, -σαν**:

**ἔλυσα → λύσας, λύσασα, λῦσαν** (*having loosed, after loosing*)

| | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter |
|-|-----------|----------|--------|
| Nom. Sg. | λύ**σας** | λύ**σασα** | λῦ**σαν** |
| Gen. Sg. | λύ**σαντος** | λύ**σάσης** | λύ**σαντος** |

> **Important:** The aorist participle does NOT carry the augment. The augment belongs only to the indicative mood. Always remove it when forming participles.

## Second Aorist Active Participle

The second aorist participle uses the second aorist stem + **-ών, -οῦσα, -όν** (same as present active but with the second aorist stem):

**ἔβαλον → βαλών, βαλοῦσα, βαλόν** (*having thrown*)

## Aorist Passive Participle

Suffix: **-θείς, -θεῖσα, -θέν** (third declension masc./neut., first declension fem.)

**ἐλύθην → λυθείς, λυθεῖσα, λυθέν** (*having been loosed*)

## Genitive Absolute

A special construction where the participle and its noun are both in the **genitive case** and grammatically independent from the main clause. It usually expresses time or circumstance.

**ταῦτα δὲ αὐτοῦ εἰπόντος** — "Now when he had said these things..."  
(*αὐτοῦ* = genitive subject; *εἰπόντος* = genitive participle)

This construction appears frequently in Matthew, Luke, and Acts.
