## Why Learn Greek?

The New Testament was written in Greek — not in a translation, not in a summary, but in a living human language that carried specific nuance, grammar, and vocabulary. Every English Bible you have ever read is already an interpretation. Learning Greek lets you encounter the text one step closer to the way the authors wrote it.

This doesn't mean your English Bible is unreliable. It means that Greek study is a magnifying glass that reveals texture and precision that translation, by necessity, sometimes smooths over.

## What Is Koine Greek?

The Greek of the New Testament is called **Koine** (κοινή), which simply means "common." After Alexander the Great's conquests in the 4th century BC, Greek spread across the ancient Near East as a *lingua franca* — the shared language of trade, administration, and culture from Egypt to Persia.

Koine Greek is distinct from:
- **Classical Greek** (Homer, Plato, Thucydides) — older, more complex
- **Modern Greek** — the language spoken in Greece today, descended from Koine

Koine was the everyday language of educated people in the first century. When Paul wrote to Romans in Rome, Greeks in Corinth, or Ephesians in Asia Minor — he wrote in the same language they all understood.

## The New Testament in Greek

The 27 books of the New Testament were composed in the first century AD, roughly between 48 and 95 AD. They were written directly in Koine Greek, not translated from another language. The writers ranged from highly educated (Luke, Paul) to working-class (Peter, James), and their Greek reflects that diversity — which is itself valuable data for reading carefully.

The Greek manuscript tradition is extraordinarily well-attested: over 5,800 Greek manuscripts exist, more than any other ancient document by an enormous margin.

## What You Will Learn in This Course

By the end of BBG, you will be able to:
- Read and parse Greek nouns, adjectives, pronouns, and verbs
- Identify the grammatical function of any word in a New Testament sentence
- Use a Greek lexicon and commentary to do serious exegesis
- Read passages of the Greek New Testament with growing fluency

You are not aiming to become a classics scholar. You are aiming to read God's Word in the language it was given — and that goal is entirely achievable.

## A Word About Difficulty

Greek is not harder than it looks, but it is different. The alphabet is new. The case system is new. The verb system is new. Each of those adjustments requires patient repetition. But students around the world — with no prior language-learning experience — complete a first-year Greek course every semester. You can too.

Mounce's approach in BBG is to get you to the actual text as quickly as possible, so every vocabulary word and paradigm you learn is directly from the New Testament itself.
