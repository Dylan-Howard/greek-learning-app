## The Greek Alphabet

The Greek alphabet has **24 letters**. Most can be learned quickly, especially since many look similar to English letters. A few require more effort. Learn the name of each letter, its uppercase form, its lowercase form, and its sound.

| # | Uppercase | Lowercase | Name | Pronunciation |
|---|-----------|-----------|------|---------------|
| 1 | Α | α | Alpha | *a* as in **f**a**ther** |
| 2 | Β | β | Beta | *b* as in **b**ook |
| 3 | Γ | γ | Gamma | *g* as in **g**o; before γ, κ, χ, ξ: *ng* |
| 4 | Δ | δ | Delta | *d* as in **d**og |
| 5 | Ε | ε | Epsilon | *e* (short) as in **e**lephant |
| 6 | Ζ | ζ | Zeta | *dz* or *z* |
| 7 | Η | η | Eta | *a* (long) as in **a**te |
| 8 | Θ | θ | Theta | *th* as in **th**eme |
| 9 | Ι | ι | Iota | *i* (short) as in **i**t; (long) as in mac**hi**ne |
| 10 | Κ | κ | Kappa | *k* as in **k**ing |
| 11 | Λ | λ | Lambda | *l* as in **l**and |
| 12 | Μ | μ | Mu | *m* as in **m**an |
| 13 | Ν | ν | Nu | *n* as in **n**et |
| 14 | Ξ | ξ | Xi | *x* as in bo**x** |
| 15 | Ο | ο | Omicron | *o* (short) as in **o**ff |
| 16 | Π | π | Pi | *p* as in **p**an |
| 17 | Ρ | ρ | Rho | *r* (slightly trilled) |
| 18 | Σ | σ/ς | Sigma | *s* as in **s**un *(ς used at word end)* |
| 19 | Τ | τ | Tau | *t* as in **t**all |
| 20 | Υ | υ | Upsilon | *u* as in Fr. **tu** or Ger. **ü** |
| 21 | Φ | φ | Phi | *ph* as in **ph**one |
| 22 | Χ | χ | Chi | *ch* as in Sc. lo**ch** (breathy k) |
| 23 | Ψ | ψ | Psi | *ps* as in li**ps** |
| 24 | Ω | ω | Omega | *o* (long) as in **o**bey |

> **Sigma has two lowercase forms**: σ is used everywhere inside a word; ς is used only at the **end** of a word. Example: σάρξ (*sarx*, flesh) — note σ at the start and ς at the end.

## Vowels

Greek has **seven vowel letters**: α, ε, η, ι, ο, υ, ω.

- **Short vowels**: α (sometimes), ε, ι (sometimes), ο, υ (sometimes)
- **Long vowels**: α (sometimes), η, ι (sometimes), υ (sometimes), ω

Eta (η) is always long. Epsilon (ε) is always short. Omega (ω) is always long. Omicron (ο) is always short.

Alpha (α), iota (ι), and upsilon (υ) can be either long or short — context and vocabulary must be learned.

## Diphthongs

A **diphthong** is two vowels that blend into a single sound.

| Diphthong | Pronunciation | Example |
|-----------|--------------|---------|
| αι | *ai* as in **ai**sle | καί (*and*) |
| αυ | *au* as in **ou**t | αὐτός (*self*) |
| ει | *ei* as in **ei**ght | εἰς (*into*) |
| ευ | *eu* as in f**eu**d | εὐθύς (*immediately*) |
| οι | *oi* as in **oi**l | οἶκος (*house*) |
| ου | *ou* as in **oo**ze | οὐ (*not*) |
| υι | *ui* as in **wi**n | (rare) |

**Improper diphthongs**: When α, η, or ω appears with an **iota subscript** (ᾳ, ῃ, ῳ) the iota is written below and is usually silent in Erasmian pronunciation but affects grammar.

## Breathing Marks

Every Greek word that **begins with a vowel** (or diphthong) carries a breathing mark over the first vowel.

- **Smooth breathing** ( ᾿ ) — no additional sound: ἀπό (*from*)
- **Rough breathing** ( ῾ ) — adds an *h* sound: ὁ (*the*), pronounced *ho*

If a word begins with a diphthong, the breathing mark goes over the **second** vowel of the diphthong: αἱ, εἰ, οἱ.

Rho (ρ) at the start of a word always takes a rough breathing: ῥήτωρ (*rhetor*).

## Gamma Nasal

When **γ** appears before another **γ, κ, χ**, or **ξ**, it is pronounced like the English *ng* in si**ng**:
- ἄγγελος → *angelos* (messenger, angel)
- ἐγκαίνια → *engkainia* (dedication festival)

## Final Letters

Sigma (ς) at the end of a word, nu-movable, and the final forms of other letters will come up in vocabulary. For now, just remember: **ς** = sigma at a word's end.
