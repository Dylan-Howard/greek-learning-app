## The Three Voices

**Active**: Subject performs the action → λύω (*I loose*)  
**Middle**: Subject performs the action with personal interest or reflexive involvement → λύομαι (*I loose for myself / I loose myself*)  
**Passive**: Subject receives the action → λύομαι (*I am being loosed*)

The critical issue: in the **present and imperfect tenses**, the middle and passive have **identical forms**. Context and lexical knowledge determine whether a given form is middle or passive.

## Present Middle/Passive Endings

Add these endings to the **present stem** using the connecting vowel (ο/ε):

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| 1st | -ομαι | -όμεθα |
| 2nd | -ῃ (-εσαι contracted) | -εσθε |
| 3rd | -εται | -ονται |

**Full conjugation of λύω (middle/passive)**

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| 1st | λύ**ομαι** | λύ**όμεθα** |
| 2nd | λύ**ῃ** | λύ**εσθε** |
| 3rd | λύ**εται** | λύ**ονται** |

## Translating the Middle

The Greek middle expresses that the subject has a special interest in the action or is acting on themselves. English doesn't have a direct equivalent, so translations vary:
- Reflexive: "I loose myself"
- Intensive: "I myself loose"
- Sometimes it adds nuance that English simply expresses with the active

In practice, many verbs that look middle in form function effectively as active in translation. Learn how each verb functions as you encounter it.

## Deponent Verbs

A **deponent verb** is one that has **only middle or passive forms** in the lexicon, but is **translated as active**. About 75 verbs in the NT are deponent. Their lexical form ends in -ομαι.

Examples of common deponent verbs:
- **ἔρχομαι** — I come, I go (not "I am being come")
- **πορεύομαι** — I go, I travel
- **ἀποκρίνομαι** — I answer
- **γίνομαι** — I become, I am

Deponency is a lexical fact — you must learn which verbs are deponent as you encounter them.
