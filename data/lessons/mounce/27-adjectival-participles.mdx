## Adjectival vs. Adverbial Participles

We have seen participles used as **adverbs** (modifying the main verb). Participles can also be used as **adjectives** (modifying a noun) or as **nouns** (substantive participles).

The **presence or absence of the article** is the key signal:

| Use | Article? | Example |
|-----|----------|---------|
| Adverbial | No article | λύων εἶπεν... (*While loosing, he said...*)  |
| Adjectival | Article before participle | ὁ **λύων** ἄνθρωπος (*the loosing man*) |
| Substantive | Article, no noun | ὁ **λύων** (*the one who is loosing*) |

## The Adjectival Participle

An adjectival participle modifies a noun directly. It follows **attributive position** (article + participle + noun, or article + noun + article + participle).

- ὁ **πιστεύων** ἄνθρωπος — "the believing man"
- τὸ **γεγραμμένον** — "the thing that has been written"

The participle must agree with the noun in gender, number, and case.

## The Substantive Participle

When the article + participle stands without a noun, the participle functions as a **noun**.

- ὁ **πιστεύων** — "the one who believes" / "the believer" (masculine)
- οἱ **πιστεύοντες** — "those who believe" / "the believers" (masculine plural)
- τὸ **πιστευόμενον** — "the thing believed" (neuter)

This is extremely common in John's Gospel and the epistles:
- John 3:16: ὁ **πιστεύων** εἰς αὐτόν — "everyone who believes in him"
- John 3:36: ὁ **πιστεύων** εἰς τὸν υἱόν — "the one who believes in the Son"

## Perfect Participle

The **perfect participle** expresses a completed action with ongoing results. Adjectivally used, it often describes a resulting state:

- ὁ **γεγραμμένος** νόμος — "the written law" (it was written and stands written)
- γέγραπται → γεγραμμένος, -η, -ον (*written*)
