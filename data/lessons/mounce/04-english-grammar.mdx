## Why English Grammar Matters Here

To learn Greek grammar, you need a shared vocabulary for talking about grammar. Most students learned some of this in school but have forgotten it. This lesson reviews the key concepts — not as an English lesson, but as preparation for applying them to Greek.

## Parts of Speech

| Part of Speech | Definition | English Example |
|---------------|-----------|----------------|
| **Noun** | Names a person, place, thing, or idea | *God, love, Jerusalem* |
| **Verb** | Expresses action or state of being | *speaks, is, gives* |
| **Adjective** | Modifies a noun | *holy, eternal, great* |
| **Adverb** | Modifies a verb, adjective, or adverb | *truly, already, not* |
| **Pronoun** | Stands in place of a noun | *he, she, they, it* |
| **Preposition** | Shows relationship between words | *in, from, with, through* |
| **Conjunction** | Connects words, phrases, or clauses | *and, but, because* |
| **Article** | Marks a noun as definite or indefinite | *the, a/an* |
| **Particle** | A small connecting or qualifying word | *indeed, yet, also* |

## Case

In Greek, nouns **change their ending** to show their function in a sentence. This is called **case**. English mostly shows case through word order; Greek shows it through inflection (ending changes).

Greek has **five cases** (you'll learn them in detail in chapters 6 and 7):

| Case | Function | English equivalent |
|------|----------|-------------------|
| **Nominative** | Subject of the verb | "God" in "**God** loves" |
| **Genitive** | Possession or relationship | "of God" or "God's" |
| **Dative** | Indirect object; means/location | "to God," "for God" |
| **Accusative** | Direct object | "God" in "I love **God**" |
| **Vocative** | Direct address | "O God!" |

In English: "The **woman** sees **the man**" — you know *woman* is the subject and *man* is the object from word order. In Greek, you know it from the **ending** of each noun, so word order is much more flexible.

## Number and Gender

**Number** is singular or plural. Greek nouns, adjectives, and verbs all show number.

**Gender** in Greek is grammatical, not necessarily biological. Every Greek noun is masculine, feminine, or neuter. You must learn the gender of each vocabulary word. It matters because adjectives and articles **must agree** in gender with the noun they modify.

Examples:
- ὁ λόγος (the word) — masculine
- ἡ ἀρχή (the beginning) — feminine
- τὸ εὐαγγέλιον (the gospel) — neuter

## Person in Verbs

Greek verbs encode **person** and **number** directly in their endings:
- **First person**: the speaker (I, we)
- **Second person**: the one spoken to (you, you all)
- **Third person**: the one spoken about (he, she, it, they)

So λύω means "I loose" — the ending ω tells you the subject is first person singular.

## Tense, Voice, and Mood

**Tense** in Greek is more about **aspect** (the *kind* of action) than strictly time:
- **Present**: ongoing, continuous action
- **Aorist**: simple, point-in-time action
- **Perfect**: completed action with ongoing results

**Voice** describes the relationship of the subject to the action:
- **Active**: subject performs the action (he loosens)
- **Middle**: subject performs the action with some personal interest (he loosens *for himself*)
- **Passive**: subject receives the action (he is loosened)

**Mood** describes the relationship of the verb to reality:
- **Indicative**: a statement of fact (he loosens)
- **Subjunctive**: possibility or contingency (if he loosens)
- **Imperative**: a command (Loosen!)
- **Optative**: a wish (may he loosen)
