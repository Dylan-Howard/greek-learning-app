## The Relative Pronoun

The relative pronoun introduces a **relative clause** — a subordinate clause that modifies a noun (its antecedent). In English: "the man **who** spoke," "the word **which** he said," "the city **in which** they lived."

The relative pronoun is **ὅς, ἥ, ὅ** (*who, which, that*).

### Key Rule: Agreement and Function

The relative pronoun **agrees with its antecedent** in **gender and number** (just like an adjective). But it takes its **case** from its **function within the relative clause** — not from its antecedent.

Example:  
ὁ ἄνθρωπος **ὃν** εἶδον — "the man **whom** I saw"  
- ἄνθρωπος is masculine singular → ὃν is masculine singular ✓  
- "whom" is the **direct object** inside the relative clause → accusative ✓

### Declining ὅς, ἥ, ὅ

| Case | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter |
|------|-----------|----------|--------|
| Nom. Sg. | ὅς | ἥ | ὅ |
| Gen. Sg. | οὗ | ἧς | οὗ |
| Dat. Sg. | ᾧ | ᾗ | ᾧ |
| Acc. Sg. | ὅν | ἥν | ὅ |
| Nom. Pl. | οἵ | αἵ | ἅ |
| Gen. Pl. | ὧν | ὧν | ὧν |
| Dat. Pl. | οἷς | αἷς | οἷς |
| Acc. Pl. | οὕς | ἅς | ἅ |

> The relative pronoun looks nearly identical to the article — but notice every form of the relative pronoun has a **rough breathing** or an accent that the article lacks. The article never has an accent (it's proclitic) except τίς.

### Translation Steps for Relative Clauses

1. Identify the relative pronoun and its antecedent.
2. Note the **gender and number** of the antecedent (agrees with pronoun).
3. Determine the **case** of the relative pronoun — that's its role in the clause.
4. Translate the relative clause with "who," "which," or "that."
