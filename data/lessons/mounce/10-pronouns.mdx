## Personal Pronouns in Greek

Greek **encodes person and number in verb endings**, so a separate pronoun subject is often unnecessary. When a pronoun IS written as the subject, it is **emphatic** — the author is drawing special attention to the subject.

- λύω — "I loose" (pronoun implied in the ending)
- **ἐγὼ** λύω — "**I** loose" (emphatic: *I* am the one loosing, not someone else)

This means that whenever you see an explicit pronoun subject in Greek, pay attention — it carries extra weight.

## First Person Pronouns: ἐγώ / ἡμεῖς

| Case | Singular | Plural |
|------|----------|--------|
| Nominative | ἐγώ (*I*) | ἡμεῖς (*we*) |
| Genitive | ἐμοῦ / μου (*my, of me*) | ἡμῶν (*our, of us*) |
| Dative | ἐμοί / μοι (*to/for me*) | ἡμῖν (*to/for us*) |
| Accusative | ἐμέ / με (*me*) | ἡμᾶς (*us*) |

> The singular has two forms: a **full** (emphatic) form (ἐμοῦ, ἐμοί, ἐμέ) and a short **enclitic** form (μου, μοι, με). The enclitic is more common; the full form is emphatic.

## Second Person Pronouns: σύ / ὑμεῖς

| Case | Singular | Plural |
|------|----------|--------|
| Nominative | σύ (*you*) | ὑμεῖς (*you all*) |
| Genitive | σοῦ / σου (*your, of you*) | ὑμῶν (*your, of you all*) |
| Dative | σοί / σοι (*to/for you*) | ὑμῖν (*to/for you all*) |
| Accusative | σέ / σε (*you*) | ὑμᾶς (*you all*) |

Same pattern: full emphatic form vs. short enclitic form in the singular.

## The Genitive of Possession

The genitive of a pronoun expresses possession:
- ὁ πατήρ **μου** — "my father" (the father of me)
- ὁ λόγος **ὑμῶν** — "your word" (the word of you all)

The genitive pronoun typically follows the noun it modifies, inside the article-noun unit or after it.
