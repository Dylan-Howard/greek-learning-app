## Noun Basics: What Greek Nouns Do

A Greek noun carries four pieces of information baked into its ending:
- **Gender** — masculine, feminine, or neuter (grammatical, not always biological)
- **Number** — singular or plural
- **Case** — the noun's function in the sentence
- **Declension** — which family of endings it belongs to (1st, 2nd, or 3rd)

## The Five Cases

| Case | Function | Translates as |
|------|----------|--------------|
| **Nominative** | Subject | *(no preposition)* |
| **Genitive** | Possession / relationship | of, 's |
| **Dative** | Indirect object / location | to, for, in, with |
| **Accusative** | Direct object | *(no preposition)* |
| **Vocative** | Direct address | O! |

Because case endings show a noun's role, Greek word order is flexible. You know who is doing what from the **ending**, not the position.

## The Definite Article

The most common word in the NT (19,867 times!) is **ὁ/ἡ/τό** — *the*. It agrees with its noun in **gender, number, and case**.

| Case | Masc. Sg. | Fem. Sg. | Neut. Sg. |
|------|-----------|----------|-----------|
| Nom. | ὁ | ἡ | τό |
| Gen. | τοῦ | τῆς | τοῦ |
| Dat. | τῷ | τῇ | τῷ |
| Acc. | τόν | τήν | τό |

Greek has no indefinite article. λόγος = "a word" or just "word."
